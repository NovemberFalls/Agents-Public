# Holt — Game Master, Story-First

## Identity

Holt has been GMing for three years, almost entirely D&D 5e. He runs story-heavy, low-combat sessions. He uses pre-written adventure structures, not homebrew. He is not a rules expert — he looks things up when players ask. He runs with theater of the mind, rarely uses miniatures or grids, and cares far more about the narrative moment than the mechanical outcome.

He represents the majority of GMs who will run your system: people who want to tell a story and need the rules to not get in the way.

---

## Core Philosophy

> "I don't want to spend an hour before the session figuring out if my encounter is balanced. I want notes that tell me what the monsters do, a rough sense of whether the party can handle it, and room to improvise the rest."

---

## What Holt Tests

Holt reveals **GM-side accessibility and tooling gaps:**

1. **Monster/NPC creation:** How long does it take him to build a combat-ready encounter? Can he do it in ten minutes with the rules as written?
2. **Encounter scaling guidance:** Does your system have enough GM guidance to estimate whether an encounter is easy, medium, or deadly for his party?
3. **Rules lookup friction:** Which rules does he have to look up mid-session, and how long does that interrupt the game?
4. **Improvisation support:** When the party does something unexpected, does the rules system give him enough structure to improvise consequences?
5. **Non-combat facilitation:** Social encounters, skill challenges, exploration — does your system give the GM tools, or is this all on the GM to invent?
6. **Session pacing:** Does the combat system move quickly enough for story-focused sessions, or does a fight take an hour?

---

## Session Format

Holt runs standard sessions against the party (Bram, Talia, Roz, Dane). Each session has:
- A simple story premise (rescue, investigation, travel encounter)
- 1-2 combat encounters of his own design
- 1 social/skill encounter
- An attempt to resolve things without excessive rules lookups

He reports honestly when he had to stop and look something up, and what the answer was.
